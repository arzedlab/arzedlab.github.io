In today's post I propose to continue talking about the current problems of young people in the digital environment and talk about a well-known, but no less common and painful topic - cyberbullying, to analyze its essence, causes and ways to protect it.
Cyberbullying is very similar to its "offline bullying" - bullying or simply put, harassment. It is verbal abuse directed towards a digital user. It takes the form of threats, shame, criticism of appearance, intelligence, and so on.  All of us, especially teenagers and young adults, can be overly sensitive. It is not uncommon to ask: How can you tell the difference between cyberbullying and a bad joke? After all, sometimes a statement may not actually be bullying. It happens that we can hear unpleasant or even offensive jokes from a virtual friend. But if it is not a "one-time action", but the manner of communication, bringing discomfort, you should ask the companion to stop it. If the "player" apologizes and changes his behavior, it is likely that his actions were not intentional harassment on the Web. Otherwise we may call of cyberbullying. Here we should add that insults on the Internet, even made in private messages, are administratively punishable. It is worth learning to distinguish insults from slander and defamation, as they carry different penalties.

What does cyberbullying mean?
Cyberbullying is a generalizing concept for many threats to information and attitudinal security of a person in the digital space. There is no unified position on the number and content of cyberbullying characteristics, but we can distinguish the most common ones. Intentional - as a rule, the offenders know what makes the victim uncomfortable, or they try to find out during the first contacts. Systematic - it is the regular attacks that cause the victim to take the abuser's actions seriously, take them to heart and respond, which leads to a "chain" of harassment.
Causing harm - the victim feels emotionally, psychologically, and sometimes physically hurt. The use of digital tools-unflattering messages, photos, videos, comments, and so on.

According to the statistics, 58% of people have dealt with cyberbullying. At the same time, many perceive it as an independent security threat and a separate destructive phenomenon in the digital environment.

In general, cyberbullying can include:
- Exclusion (ostracism) - the analogue of boycott, excluding a person from a particular social group, ignoring in games, social networks, forums, etc;
- Leak (disclosure of personal data) - publication of income and expenses, intimate photos stolen from gadgets. Threats to publish personal information are also included.
- Expression of disrespect (defamation) -  attempt to blacken one's reputation, spreading rumors and gossip. The aggressor generates any information that will make the victim look "bad". Cyberbullying can also take the form of beating, grifting, account hacking and theft, cyberstalking, and so on.

What are the consequences of cyberbullying?
Many people think cyberbullying is not a serious problem, but it can cause very real physical and mental suffering. According to the researches, teenagers feel 2.5 times more stressed by online bullying. Therefore, successful cyberbullying can have a hidden but very serious negative impact on a young person's psyche. It can come to suicide attempts and self-harm. Subsequently, the victim may become the aggressor. As a result, cyberbullying is a significant issue that must be handled.

How do I prevent cyberbullying?

Often the aggressor is a peculiar type of person. Often he himself has been bullied in the past and now takes revenge. However, sometimes a user can provoke cyberbullying, for example, by starting or getting into a virtual altercation first. Sometimes it's enough just to keep the fire burning. Be digital-friendly, watch what you post, and analyze the profiles and behavior of those you interact with.
If you do encounter cyberbullying:
1. Don't respond to the attacks.
2. Take screenshots. You may utilize them to hold the bully responsible.
3. Block the aggressor's account.
4. Complain about his profile or the content that violates your boundaries - this can be done on all social networks.
5. If necessary, file a report with law enforcement.

This can and should be done if the aggressor's actions fall within the scope of an offense. Under certain circumstances aggressors can be held civilly, administratively or even criminally responsible. Do not be afraid to defend your rights. The smallest and seemingly insignificant problems can greatly interfere with our lives. We must learn to deal with them consciously and promptly.
